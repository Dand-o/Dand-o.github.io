Task 2 - narrowing down, asking questions and finding sources.

What questions do I want my website to answer?
- What is misinformation?
- How can we identify it?
- What are examples of misinformation?
- The rise in misinformation.

In the last few years we have had a lot of world events unfolding infront of our eyes, all through the use of social media. We are in a time where we can log into Twitter/X and get real time information from people on the ground, faster than ever before. Much of the research over the last few years (2020-2023) will cover events such as the COVID-19 pandemic and Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Using the literature from these events, we can hopefully itentify trends in how misinformation spreads on social media, and provide an insight on how we can combat it, using skills and knowledge on what to look for whenever we face a global event that is being broadcast online.

Notice a mention of "fake-news" in this as a related search? - seems like this may be something I may have to address in the website as this is a common search term, and is linked to a lot of negative connotations such as Trump and conspiricies.

"Who spreads misinformation on social media" is an interesting search, and in research there is evidence to show that it is in fact the user that has a responsibility to prevent and stop the spread (Khan and Idris, 2019).

I think this is an important topic to cover, especially with the massive changes on Twitter/X. With Twitter previously being a massive source of real-time information on unfolding news stories, the policies in place previously gave users a feeling that the information they were consuming on the website could be trusted to a degree, with less risk of misinformation from bots and more policies in place to ensure the spread of misinformation was reduced.
Although some of these tools are still in place (community notes) there is reason to be concerned about the rise in information on the social media platform. In light of recent breaking stories on the platform, we can analyse and compare how the changes have affected the quality of information being spread.


Audience:

People who use Twitter/X or online apps and the internet in general as a source for real-time information may be interested in this topic, but also, those who may be less literate in information online. I want my audience to be of the younger generation, and those who are guardians of younger folk, so they can navigate and use the resource as well, to help them understand the importance of information literacy online and pass on the knowledge to their children or children they take care of, be it teachers, lecturers or parents. I want to create a project that educates and challenges people on their bias and ensure people are thinking about which sources they consume and engage their thinking before taking the information they seek online as fact initially.

By presenting this through a multimedia format, I feel like I can engage an audience to participate in making choices and informing the user why their choice was correct/incorrect. For example: Allowing a user to select between two images of the same news story, one is misinformation, one is legit. By selecting the image they feel is correct, we can educate on why their decision was right or wrong, and explain in detail what they should look for if need be. What is misinformation? - How can we identify it? - What are examples of misinformation? - The rise in misinformation.

One of the ideas I have had, especially given my target audience is of the younger generations, between 12-18 years old, is the use of an interactive element that mimics the experience of TikTok. And infinite scroll type of idea in which a narrative is presented to them, as they use their scroll wheel to find out more, usuing attractive visuals, sounds and images to entice the user to want to see what comes next, whilst also educating them on the topic.
