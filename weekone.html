Task 2 - narrowing down, asking questions and finding sources.

What questions do I want my website to answer?
- What is misinformation?
- How can we identify it?
- What are examples of misinformation?
- The rise in misinformation.

In the last few years we have had a lot of world events unfolding infront of our eyes, all through the use of social media. We are in a time where we can log into Twitter/X and get real time information from people on the ground, faster than ever before. Much of the research over the last few years (2020-2023) will cover events such as the COVID-19 pandemic and Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Using the literature from these events, we can hopefully itentify trends in how misinformation spreads on social media, and provide an insight on how we can combat it, using skills and knowledge on what to look for whenever we face a global event that is being broadcast online.

It is important to use advanced searches when using the University of Glasgow's online library search to narrow down my results to academic sources and reputable websites.

I think this is an important topic to cover, especially with the massive changes on Twitter/X. With Twitter previously being a massive source of real-time information on unfolding news stories, the policies in place previously gave users a feeling that the information they were consuming on the website could be trusted to a degree, with less risk of misinformation from bots and more policies in place to ensure the spread of misinformation was reduced.
Although some of these tools are still in place (community notes) there is reason to be concerned about the rise in information on the social media platform. In light of recent breaking stories on the platform, we can analyse and compare how the changes have affected the quality of information being spread.

Audience:
People who use Twitter/X as a source for real-time information may be interested in this topic, but also, those who may be less literate in information online. I want my audience to be as broad as possible, from 13+, and to include everyone up to a university level education. I want to create a project that educates and challenges people on their bias and ensure people are thinking about which sources they consume and engage their thinking before taking the information they seek online as fact initially.

By presenting this through a multimedia format, I feel like I can engage an audience to participate in making choices and informing the user why their choice was correct/incorrect. For example: Allowing a user to select between two images of the same news story, one is misinformation, one is legit. By selecting the image they feel is correct, we can educate on why their decision was right or wrong, and explain in detail what they should look for if need be.
